Lesson 12.1

Momentum & Linear Momentum

Linear momentum (often just called momentum) quantifies the motion of an object in translational or straight-line motion. Physicists describe it as the inertia of a moving object: the greater an object's momentum, the harder it is to change its motion.

𝒑⃗ = m𝒗⃗     [Eq. 1]
𝒑⃗ = momentum (vector)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  𝒗⃗ = velocity (m/s)
SI unit of momentum: kg·m/s (no special name)
Vector quantity: Momentum always points in the same direction as velocity. It can be split into horizontal and vertical components:
𝒑ₓ = m𝒗ₓ    𝒑ᵧ = m𝒗ᵧ

Momentum and Kinetic Energy

Since both momentum and kinetic energy involve mass and velocity, they are related. Starting from KE = ½mv² and substituting p = mv:

KE = p² / (2m)     [Eq. 7]
Kinetic energy can be expressed entirely in terms of momentum and mass.
💡 Intuition
A massive truck and a fast sports car can have the same momentum if the car's velocity compensates for the truck's extra mass. Either mass or speed (or both) contribute to momentum.
Sample Problem 1 — Swallow in Flight Lesson 12.1

What is the magnitude of the momentum of a 28 g swallow flying at 8.4 m/s?

Given:
m = 28 g = 0.028 kg
v = 8.4 m/s
Solution:
  1. Use the momentum formula: 𝒑 = m𝒗
  2. Substitute: 𝒑 = (0.028 kg)(8.4 m/s)
Answer: 𝒑 = 0.24 kg·m/s  (in the direction of flight)
Sample Problem 2 — Truck vs. SUV Lesson 12.1

A 1.0 × 10⁴ kg truck travels at 12.0 m/s. (a) Find its momentum. (b) What speed must a 2.0 × 10³ kg SUV reach to have the same momentum?

Given:
m_truck = 1.0 × 10⁴ kg  |  v_truck = 12.0 m/s
m_SUV = 2.0 × 10³ kg
Part (a) — Truck's momentum:
  1. 𝒑 = m𝒗 = (1.0 × 10⁴ kg)(12.0 m/s)
𝒑_truck = 1.2 × 10⁵ kg·m/s
Part (b) — Required SUV velocity:
  1. Set p_SUV = p_truck → mv = 1.2 × 10⁵
  2. v = p / m = (1.2 × 10⁵) / (2.0 × 10³)
Answer: v_SUV = 60.0 m/s  (much faster than the truck!)
Lesson 12.2

Impulse & the Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Newton originally stated his Second Law in terms of momentum: the net force on an object equals its time rate of change of momentum.

𝑭⃗_net = Δ𝒑⃗ / Δt     [Eq. 4]
Rearranging: the product of force × time creates a change in momentum.

Impulse Defined

Impulse (J⃗) is the product of the net force and the time interval over which it acts. Its SI unit is the newton-second (N·s), which is equivalent to kg·m/s.

𝑱⃗ = 𝑭⃗_net · Δt     [Impulse definition]
𝑱⃗ = Δ𝒑⃗ = m·Δ𝒗⃗     [Impulse-Momentum Theorem]
The impulse experienced by an object equals its change in momentum.
Key Insight: For the same change in momentum, a longer contact time means a smaller average force, and vice versa. This is why catching an egg with arms pulled back (longer Δt) prevents it from breaking.

Real-World Applications

🥊
Boxing

Short contact time → large force. Agility (quick punches) matters as much as strength.

🏃
Long Jump

Bending knees on landing extends Δt, reducing the impact force on joints.

🎾
Tennis / Baseball

Following through increases contact time, building greater momentum change in the ball.

🥚
Egg Catch Game

Step backward as you catch to increase contact time and avoid breaking the egg.

🚗
Airbags

Inflate to increase the stopping time of a passenger, dramatically lowering the impact force.

Sample Problem 1 — Ball Bouncing off Wall Lesson 12.2

A 0.40 kg ball moves left at 30.0 m/s, hits a wall, and rebounds right at 20.0 m/s. Contact time = 0.010 s. Find (a) impulse, (b) average force.

Given:
m = 0.40 kg
v_i = −30.0 m/s (left = negative)
v_f = +20.0 m/s (right = positive)
Δt = 0.010 s
Part (a) — Impulse:
  1. J = mΔv = m(v_f − v_i)
  2. J = (0.40)(20.0 − (−30.0))
  3. J = (0.40)(50.0) = 20.0 N·s
The positive sign confirms the impulse (and force from wall) is directed rightward.

Part (b) — Average Force:
  1. F_net = J / Δt = 20.0 / 0.010
Answers: (a) J = 20.0 N·s  |  (b) F = 2.0 × 10³ N (rightward)
Sample Problem 2 — Vehicle Stopping Comparison Lesson 12.2

A 2200 kg vehicle moving at 26.0 m/s is stopped in: (a) 21.0 s (gentle braking), (b) 3.8 s (hard braking), (c) 0.22 s (concrete wall). Find the average force in each case.

m = 2200 kg  |  v_i = 26.0 m/s  |  v_f = 0 m/s
Formula: F_net = mΔv / Δt = (2200)(0 − 26) / Δt
  1. (a) Δt = 21.0 s  →  F = −2.7 × 10³ N
  2. (b) Δt = 3.8 s  →  F = −1.5 × 10⁴ N
  3. (c) Δt = 0.22 s  →  F = −2.6 × 10⁵ N
Negative sign = force opposes motion (stopping force).
Lesson: Shorter contact time → enormously larger force. A wall collision (0.22 s) produces ~100× more force than gentle braking (21.0 s) for the same momentum change!
Lesson 12.3

Conservation of Momentum

From Newton's Third Law, when two objects interact (collide), they exert equal and opposite forces on each other. This means their impulses are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction — so their momentum changes cancel out.

If 𝑭⃗_net,external = 0  →  Δ𝒑⃗_total = 0  →  𝒑⃗_total,i = 𝒑⃗_total,f
Law of Conservation of Momentum: In the absence of a net external force, the total momentum of a closed system remains unchanged.
🔑 Critical Condition
Only external forces change a system's total momentum. Forces between objects within the system (internal forces) cancel each other out — they do NOT change total momentum. The billiard-table's normal force cancels gravity; the forces between the balls are internal.

Cannon-Ball System Illustration

Before firing: both barrel and ball are at rest → total momentum = 0. After firing: ball moves forward, barrel recoils backward with the same magnitude of momentum. Total momentum is still 0. This is why rockets work in empty space!

Inelastic Collision & Conservation

Even when colliding objects deform, generate heat, or stick together, momentum is still conserved as long as there is no net external force. The type of collision only affects whether kinetic energy is conserved.

Sample Problem — Big Fish Eats Small Fish Lesson 12.3

A 5 kg big fish swims at 1 m/s (right) toward a 1 kg small fish at rest. After the big fish swallows the small fish, what is the combined velocity?

m₁ = 5 kg, v₁ = 1 m/s  |  m₂ = 1 kg, v₂ = 0 m/s
Apply Conservation of Momentum:
  1. Total momentum before = total momentum after
  2. (5)(1) + (1)(0) = (5 + 1) · v_after
  3. 5 = 6 · v_after
  4. v_after = 5/6 ≈ 0.833 m/s
Answer: v_after = 5/6 m/s ≈ 0.833 m/s (rightward) — slower than before because mass increased.
Sample Problem — Gliding Carts on Air Track Lesson 12.3

A 0.5 kg gliding cart bumps into and sticks to a stationary 1.5 kg cart. How fast do the coupled carts move after collision?

m₁ = 0.5 kg, v_before = v_before  |  m₂ = 1.5 kg, v₂ = 0
  1. (0.5)v_before = (0.5 + 1.5) · v_after
  2. v_after = (0.5 / 2.0) · v_before
Answer: v_after = ¼ v_before — the combined mass is 4× the original, so speed drops to one-quarter.
Lesson 12.4.1

Inelastic Collisions

A collision is inelastic when kinetic energy is not conserved — some is converted to deformation, heat, or sound. Momentum is still conserved (as always, if no net external force).

PropertyInelasticPerfectly Inelastic
MomentumConserved ✓Conserved ✓
Kinetic EnergyNOT conserved ✗NOT conserved ✗ (maximum loss)
Objects afterSeparate with different velocitiesStick together (move as one)
Formulam₁v₁ᵢ + m₂v₂ᵢ = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂fm₁v₁ᵢ + m₂v₂ᵢ = (m₁ + m₂)v_f
Common Mistake: Students often assume momentum is not conserved in inelastic collisions. It always is, as long as there are no net external forces. What changes is kinetic energy.

Key Observations from the Virtual Lab (Part A)

  • 1Total velocity changes: The sum of velocities before and after collision are generally not equal. The carts merge and slow down.
  • 2Total momentum is conserved: p₁ᵢ + p₂ᵢ = p₁f + p₂f even though objects stick together.
  • 3Momentum transfer is equal & opposite: The momentum gained by cart 2 equals the momentum lost by cart 1.
  • 4Kinetic energy DECREASES: Some KE is lost to deformation/heat. KE_before > KE_after.
  • 5Elasticity = 0.5 (intermediate): Objects separate (unlike perfectly inelastic), less KE is lost than perfectly inelastic, but more than elastic. Momentum still conserved.
Lesson 12.4.2

Elastic Collisions

A collision is elastic when both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. No energy is lost to permanent deformation or heat. Ideal elastic collisions occur in atomic/subatomic interactions and are approximated by billiard balls and gas molecules.

Conservation of Momentum: m_A·v_A + m_B·v_B = m_A·v'_A + m_B·v'_B
Conservation of KE: ½m_A·v_A² + ½m_B·v_B² = ½m_A·v'_A² + ½m_B·v'_B²
Both equations must be satisfied simultaneously in elastic collisions.

The Relative Velocity Condition (Key Feature)

Combining both conservation laws for elastic collisions yields a powerful result:

v_A − v_B = v'_B − v'_A     [Eqn 5]
The relative velocity of approach equals the relative velocity of separation (but opposite in sign). This is the defining characteristic of elastic collisions.

Special Case: Equal Masses

When m_A = m_B, solving both equations simultaneously gives:

v'_A = v_B     and     v'_B = v_A
Equal-mass objects simply swap velocities in an elastic collision. If one is at rest, it moves and the other stops completely.

Key Observations from the Virtual Lab (Parts B–D)

  • 1Velocity swap (equal mass only): Carts exchange velocities. If masses differ, they do NOT simply swap.
  • 2Momentum swap (equal mass only): Since p = mv and masses are equal, swapping velocities swaps momentum too.
  • 3Direction reversal not always: A cart doesn't always reverse direction — it depends on the mass ratio and initial velocities.
  • 4Total velocity sum changes: Unlike momentum, the sum of individual velocities is generally not conserved.
  • 5Total momentum is conserved: Always. p_total,before = p_total,after.
  • 6Momentum transfers are equal & opposite: Same as inelastic — Newton's Third Law governs this.
  • 7Total kinetic energy is conserved: KE_before = KE_after. This is the key distinction from inelastic collisions.
Sample Problem 1 — Equal-Mass Billiard Balls Lesson 12.4.2

Ball A (mass m) moves at v_A; Ball B (mass m) moves at v_B. Elastic collision. Find v'_A and v'_B.

m_A = m_B = m (same mass)
Using both conservation equations:
  1. Momentum: v_A + v_B = v'_A + v'_B
  2. Relative velocity: v_A − v_B = v'_B − v'_A
  3. Add the two equations: 2v_A = 2v'_B → v'_B = v_A
  4. Subtract: 2v_B = 2v'_A → v'_A = v_B
Answer: The balls swap velocities. v'_A = v_B and v'_B = v_A. If B was at rest (v_B = 0): A stops and B moves with A's original speed.
Sample Problem 2 — Gliders on Air Track (Different Masses) Lesson 12.4.2

Glider A: m_A = 0.150 kg, moving right at 0.800 m/s. Glider B: m_B = 0.300 kg, moving left at 2.20 m/s. Elastic collision. Find final velocities.

v_A = +0.800 m/s (right)  |  v_B = −2.20 m/s (left)
Set up two equations:
  1. Momentum: (0.150)(0.800) + (0.300)(−2.20) = 0.150·v'_A + 0.300·v'_B
  2. Simplify: 0.120 − 0.660 = 0.150·v'_A + 0.300·v'_B
  3. −0.540 = 0.150·v'_A + 0.300·v'_B  ···(i)
  4. Relative velocity: v_A − v_B = v'_B − v'_A
  5. 0.800 − (−2.20) = v'_B − v'_A
  6. 3.00 = v'_B − v'_A  ···(ii)
  7. From (ii): v'_B = v'_A + 3.00. Sub into (i):
  8. −0.540 = 0.150·v'_A + 0.300·(v'_A + 3.00)
  9. −0.540 = 0.450·v'_A + 0.900 → 0.450·v'_A = −1.44
  10. v'_A = −3.20 m/s  (leftward)
  11. v'_B = −3.20 + 3.00 = −0.200 m/s  (leftward)
Answer: v'_A = −3.20 m/s (A reverses and goes left)  |  v'_B = −0.200 m/s (B slows but continues left)
Sample Problem 3 — Proton–Helium Collision Lesson 12.4.2

A proton (m_p = 1.01 u) moves at 3.60 × 10⁴ m/s and elastically collides head-on with a helium nucleus (m_He = 4.00 u) initially at rest.

v_p = 3.60 × 10⁴ m/s  |  v_He = 0
Using elastic collision formulas for unequal masses:
  1. v'_p = [(m_p − m_He)/(m_p + m_He)] · v_p
  2. v'_p = [(1.01 − 4.00)/(1.01 + 4.00)] · 3.60×10⁴
  3. v'_p = [−2.99/5.01] · 3.60×10⁴ ≈ −2.15×10⁴ m/s
  4. v'_He = [2m_p/(m_p + m_He)] · v_p
  5. v'_He = [2(1.01)/5.01] · 3.60×10⁴ ≈ +1.45×10⁴ m/s
Answer: Proton bounces back at ≈ 2.15 × 10⁴ m/s; Helium moves forward at ≈ 1.45 × 10⁴ m/s.

Quick Review

Key Takeaways & Formula Sheet

📐 All Essential Formulas

  • Momentum: 𝒑⃗ = m𝒗⃗  (kg·m/s)
  • Components: pₓ = mvₓ ; pᵧ = mvᵧ
  • KE–momentum relation: KE = p² / 2m
  • Net force (Newton's 2nd): F⃗_net = Δp⃗/Δt
  • Impulse: J⃗ = F⃗_net · Δt  (N·s)
  • Impulse-Momentum Theorem: J⃗ = Δp⃗ = mΔv⃗
  • Conservation of Momentum: p⃗_total,i = p⃗_total,f (when F_net,ext = 0)
  • Perfectly inelastic: (m₁+m₂)v_f = m₁v₁ᵢ + m₂v₂ᵢ
  • Elastic – momentum: m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v'₁ + m₂v'₂
  • Elastic – kinetic energy: ½m₁v₁² + ½m₂v₂² = ½m₁v'₁² + ½m₂v'₂²
  • Elastic – relative velocity: v₁ − v₂ = v'₂ − v'₁
  • Elastic – equal mass: v'₁ = v₂ and v'₂ = v₁ (velocity swap)

🧠 Conceptual Reminders

  • Momentum is a vector — direction matters! Use +/− signs consistently.
  • Momentum is always conserved in collisions with no net external force, regardless of collision type.
  • Kinetic energy is only conserved in elastic collisions.
  • Impulse = area under a Force-vs-Time graph.
  • To reduce impact force: increase contact time (airbags, bending knees, follow-through).
  • In elastic equal-mass collisions, the balls always swap velocities.
  • The Law of Conservation of Momentum applies even during radioactive decay and stellar explosions — not just collisions.
⚡ Problem-Solving Strategy
1. Define a sign convention (e.g., right = positive).   2. List all known quantities.   3. Identify collision type (elastic/inelastic).   4. Write conservation of momentum equation.   5. For elastic: add the relative-velocity equation as a second equation.   6. Solve the system of equations.   7. Check: does the answer make physical sense?
